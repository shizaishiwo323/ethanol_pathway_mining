Abstract

intermediates in the branching path following C-C coupling constrains the directed selectivity of
electroreduction CO -to-ethanol. Here, we present a subsurface Co-doped CuS (Co-Sub-CuS)
2
catalyst, which exhibits directed selectivity toward ethanol. We elucidate the role of subsurface
doping in enhancing the oxophilicity of surface Cu sites, thereby facilitating the conversion of key
intermediates (*CHCHO*) via the formation of surface-O bonds, Sguiding subsequent protonation
S
towards ethanol. Moreover, the surface sulfur vacancies cEreated by subsurface Co-doping help
R

N

I

L

directionally selective catalysts for CO electroreduction.

Computational methods
R
Density functional theory (DFT) calculations were performed using the Vienna Ab initio
P
Simulation Package (VASP) with the projector augment wave (PAW) method56. The electron
exchange and correlation energy were treatedN within the generalized gradient approximation
(GGA) and the Perdew, Bueke, and ErnzerhoIf (PBE) functional. Spin polarization was taken into
account in all calculations. The Brillouin zone was sampled using a k-points grid of 1×1×1. The
E
energy cut-off and convergence criterLia for force were set as 480 eV and 0.03 eV/Å, respectively.
Partial occupancies were determinCed using a Gaussian smearing scheme with a smearing width of
0.05 eV. The ground state strucItures of the adsorbed *COCOH, *CHCO, *CHCOH, *CHCHO,
*CHC and *OCHCH were de T termined by searching the lowest energy one among all the possible
2
R
configurations. The CuS (100) surface was modeled by six atomic layers of 72 atoms, and the
A
geometry of the upper three layers was optimized while constraining the lower three layers. The
thickness of the vacuum layer is 20 Å, and the supercell dimensions are 11.447 Å, 16.463 Å and
25.507 Å. For the Vs- CuS model, a sulfur atom in the first atomic layer of CuS (100) surface was
removed. For the Co-sub-CuS model, a copper atom in the second atomic layer of CuS-Vs surface
was replaced with a cobalt atom. Density of states were calculated and weighted by the respective
Hamilton matrix elements using the Local-Orbital Basis Suite Towards Electronic-Structure
Reconstruction software package (LOBSTER)57. The anti-bonding and bonding interactions were
analyzed based on the projected crystal orbital Hamilton population (-pCOHP) method. Changes
in reaction energy for the competing pathways leading to C products were calculated.
2
We calculated the activation barriers for asymmetric coupling of *CO and *COH to form
*OCCOH using the dimer method implemented in the CP2K code with the Quickstep module58.
The molecularly optimized Double-Zeta-Valence-Polarization (DZVP-MOLOPT) basis sets and
Goedecker-Teter-Hutter (GTH) pseudopotentials were used for core electrons in combination with
Perdew-Burke-Ernzerhof (PBE) exchange correlation functional59. All calculations were
converged with a Broyden-Fletcher-Goldfarb-Shannon (BFGS) optimizer. The periodic boundary

correction. Vibrational frequency calculation was performed to ascertain the saddle state by
